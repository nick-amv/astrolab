How to Choose a Career: A Method, Not Guesswork

"What do you want to be?" is a bad first question. It demands an answer you don't have yet. There's a more reliable way — start not with the name of a job, but with your inclinations.

In short

You don't choose a career from a list of "in-demand" jobs — you choose by matching your interests, values, and inclinations to real careers. The RIASEC model (six types of interests) compares your profile against thousands of careers and, in 15 minutes, gives you a direction — not a finished answer, but an honest hint about where to look.

Most people choose a career in one of three ways: on their parents' advice, from a list of "in-demand" specialties, or by following someone they know who "made it work." All three have one thing in common — they start on the outside, not the inside. And that's exactly why they so often end with the phrase "turned out it wasn't for me."

There's a more reliable way. It doesn't promise the one true answer — but it removes the guessing and gives you a direction based on who you are, not on what happens to be trendy right now.

Why "who to become" is the wrong first step

The name of a job is the packaging, not the contents. Behind the word "lawyer" you'll find both quiet analytical work at a desk and public arguments in a courtroom — two completely different kinds of people. When you try to choose by the name, you're guessing at what's inside. It's far more accurate to first understand which actions you enjoy, and only then look for careers where there's plenty of them.

A strong choice starts not with "who to become," but with "what I like doing" — and what I'm good at.

What the RIASEC model is and why you can trust it

RIASEC is an international career-guidance standard developed by psychologist John Holland. It describes a person's interests through six types:

  • R — Realistic: hands-on work, tools, machinery, building a result you can touch.
  • I — Investigative: analysis, logic, "why is it built this way."
  • A — Artistic: creativity, imagery, design, the freedom to invent.
  • S — Social: helping, teaching, caring, working with people.
  • E — Enterprising: leadership, persuasion, initiative, running your own thing.
  • C — Conventional: order, data, precision, working within a system.

The value of the model is that it isn't floating in the air: the international occupation database O*NET tags thousands of real careers with these same codes. So your profile is compared not against a horoscope, but against actual careers — and it shows you where the match is strongest.

Interests are half of it. The other half is values

Interests tell you what you like doing. Values tell you what for. The very same social interest will lead different people to different places: one needs stability, another freedom and independence, a third recognition. That's why good career guidance doesn't ask "does this matter to you?" (almost everyone says yes) — it forces you to choose between two values. That's how you see what's truly a priority for you.

But what about salary?

Money isn't the first question, but it isn't the last one either. The right order is this: first the direction (where you'll be good and won't burn out), then the reality check — how much it pays and how you get in. Frequently asked questions

How do you choose a career when nothing appeals to you?

Start not with "who to become," but with which actions you enjoy: fixing, figuring things out, helping, inventing, leading, putting things in order. These inclinations (the RIASEC model) match real careers better than any list of "in-demand" jobs.

Can a test really determine your career?

A career test gives you a direction and a match, not a finished answer or a guarantee. It's an estimate based on the RIASEC model, not a diagnosis — your profile changes with experience, so it's worth retaking the test every six months.

What matters more — salary or interest?

Both, but in a certain order. Interest and inclinations decide where you'll be good and won't burn out; salary and conditions are your reality filter. First the direction, then the check against money and the path in.
