How to Know It's Time to Change Careers

It's Sunday evening and something inside is already tightening. Sound familiar? On its own that means nothing — it's how people react to a hard month and to work that stopped being theirs long ago. The trick is telling one from the other without acting rashly.

In short

It's time when the fatigue lasts for months and doesn't lift after a proper break, and your interest in the work itself — not just a particular job — has gone flat. That's different from a bad patch, and from burnout, which the WHO (ICD-11, 2019) frames as a result of workplace stress and is healed by recovery. Changing course isn't failure: people born in 1957–64 held an average of 12.7 jobs across their careers (BLS).

Start here: "I want to quit" and "it's time to change careers" are two different things, and confusing them is expensive. The first is usually about a place — the manager, the pay, the commute, the one project that drained you dry. The second is about the work itself, the thing you do for eight hours a day no matter where you sit. A job you can change in a month. A profession means years of retraining and a dip in income, so the decision deserves testing, not a verdict handed down on a Friday-night mood.

Ask yourself honestly: if the same work turned up tomorrow at a perfect company with decent pay, would the weight lift? Or would the work still feel like someone else's?

Signal one: boredom that no new task can fix

Ordinary work boredom clears the moment you pick up something fresh — a project, a role, a promotion. The worrying version is different: you've already tried the internal moves and the pull still won't come back. An old but durable frame helps here, from Amy Wrzesniewski and colleagues (Journal of Research in Personality, 1997): people relate to their work as a job for money, a career for advancement, or a calling for the work's own sake — and in their sample the three groups split roughly evenly into thirds. If you used to sit in the third group and now you're stuck in the first, and no new task shifts that, the boredom stopped being about tasks. It's about direction.

Signal two: burnout — but don't mistake it for being done with the work

In 2019 the WHO added burnout to the ICD-11 as an occupational phenomenon — not an illness, but the result of chronic, undigested workplace stress. It has three clear markers: exhaustion, a growing mental distance and cynicism toward the job, and the sense that you've stopped coping. Here's the trap: burnout disguises itself as "I hate my profession," when it actually heals through rest and a lighter load, not through switching fields.

So don't decide anything at the bottom of the pit. Recover first — a real break, sleep, the load lifted off. Then look: has your interest in the work come back or not. If it has, that was burnout, and there's no career to change. If it hasn't returned even on a rested mind, that's a signal, not a symptom of overwork.

Signal three: the work stopped adding up to meaning

The quietest of the three. No blow-ups here, no exhaustion — everything looks fine, but inside it's been empty a long time. You do your work competently and feel not a flicker of response. Per Gallup (State of the Global Workplace, 2024), just 23% of employees worldwide feel engaged at work, while 62% are "going through the motions" — the "quiet quitting" where someone is present in body but not invested. Landing in that 62% for a couple of months is normal. Spending years there is costly, and not in money.

The check is simple. Remember the last time a workday ended with the feeling that you'd done something worthwhile. If you have to strain and it was a while ago, meaning has left the work, not just a single week.

Or is it just a bad month?

Fair question, and one you have to ask. A brutal project, an awkward new boss, the crunch before a reporting deadline — all of it hits how you feel, and all of it passes. A real signal differs in two ways: it holds for months rather than weeks, and it doesn't vanish after rest. A one-off slump is a state. A steady one, month after month, is a mismatch with the direction.

Watching yourself helps. Keep a note and, once a week, give your work mood an honest score — one to five will do. After a couple of months the shape is visible: if it dipped and leveled out, that was a patch; if it slides down and won't bounce back, that's a trend, and it's worth taking seriously.

Is changing careers at 40 normal, or too late?

The word "late" is nearly always borrowed here — pulled from your head, not the facts. Median job tenure for U.S. wage and salary workers is 3.9 years, per the Bureau of Labor Statistics for 2024, the lowest since 2002. And here's the anchor I promised: from the long-running NLSY79, the same BLS counted an average of 12.7 jobs held by people born in 1957–64, by age 56. Twelve. A share of those moves landed well into their thirties and forties.

A change of course mid-way isn't a glitch in a biography — it's the ordinary grain of one. "Late" rarely comes from age. It comes from one thing: living for years with a signal you keep hiding from yourself.

What to do with this right now

  1. Separate the place from the work. Write down what's actually hard: the company, the tasks, the pay, the manager — or the profession itself. Often far less needs changing than it seems.
  2. If there are signs of burnout by the ICD-11 markers, recover first and postpone the career decision. At the bottom of the pit, every choice is distorted.
  3. Track your mood on a scale for two or three weeks. You're looking for a trend, not one bad day.
  4. Decide where you're actually headed. "Leaving somewhere" is half of it; the other half is knowing what fits you. Start there: take the free career test and look at the directions where your interests line up.

Frequently asked questions

How do I tell burnout from actually being ready to leave?

Burnout, as the WHO defines it (ICD-11, 2019), is the result of chronic workplace stress: exhaustion, cynicism toward the job, and the sense that you've stopped coping. It's healed by recovery, not by switching fields. If a proper break and a lighter load still don't bring back your interest in the work itself, that's no longer about fatigue — it's a mismatch with the direction.

At what age is it too late to change careers?

The data argues against the word "late." Per the NLSY79 (BLS), people born in 1957–64 held an average of 12.7 jobs by age 56, and a good share of those moves happened well into their thirties and beyond. Changing careers at 35, 45, or 50 is a statistical norm, not an exception. The question isn't your age — it's whether there's a real signal and where exactly you're headed.

Should I change careers over one rough patch?

No. A brutal project, a clash with your manager, or burnout after a crunch is a state, not a verdict on the work. The "it's time" signal is different: it lasts for months and doesn't lift after rest. Before you overhaul everything, honestly separate it out — do you dislike the profession, or this particular job, task, and stretch of time?
